Our Story

Who We Are

Kwetu Care Foundation is a community-based organization working to bridge healthcare gaps in rural and underserved areas of Kenya, within Laikipia County and neighbouring communities.

Founded on the belief that healthcare should be accessible, dignified, and community-centered, we bring care closer to the people who need it most.

We focus on prevention, early diagnosis, screening, and timely treatment: because a healthy community is a productive, thriving community.

Our approach combines direct medical intervention with long-term community empowerment, ensuring health is a lasting legacy.

Restoring Health and Preventing Avoidable Illness

In many rural communities, preventable and treatable illnesses continue to cause unnecessary suffering. Conditions such as infections, diarrhea, unmanaged chronic diseases, and complications from delayed care often escalate simply because help is too far away or sought too late.

For many families, accessing healthcare means traveling long distances, for some patients, it can take close to four hours just to reach the nearest facility. By the time patients arrive at a clinic, manageable conditions have often progressed into emergencies, making treatment more complicated and expensive.

Kwetu Care exists to close that gap. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), access to primary healthcare is the most efficient and effective way to achieve health for all.

Through mobile medical outreaches, we bring screening, diagnosis, and treatment directly into underserved communities. By identifying illness early and initiating timely care, we reduce complications, hospitalizations, and preventable deaths.

Our goal is simple: restore health early, before illness becomes crisis.

Maternal & Child Health

Maternal and Child health

In most rural communities, many mothers and children face preventable risks due to distance, limited resources, and lack of access to skilled care.

Complications during pregnancy and childbirth are often detectable early, yet without regular prenatal checkups, they go unnoticed until it is too late. Our mobile outreaches provide on-site antenatal care, including ultrasounds, screening for pre-eclampsia, fetal distress, and nutrition support. By identifying these risks early, we can arrange safe transport to higher-level facilities long before an emergency occurs.

In addition to prenatal care, we support postnatal follow-up, child growth monitoring, immunizations, and health education. Through these interventions, we aim to tackle the crisis of maternal mortality—an issue highlighted by the UNICEF Global Health Data as one of the most pressing health challenges in sub-Saharan Africa.

Our mission is to reduce preventable maternal and child illness, empower mothers with knowledge and care, and ensure healthy beginnings for families across rural Kenya.

Preserving Sight

Preserving Sight & Preventing Avoidable Blindness

Vision is essential to independence, productivity, and quality of life, yet preventable and treatable eye conditions continue to affect many in rural communities. Without timely screening and intervention, simple issues like refractive errors, cataracts, or infections can lead to permanent vision loss.

Many people must travel long distances to access eye care, and for some, that journey is simply not possible. As a result, conditions that could have been treated early progress to blindness, impacting individuals and their families.

At Kwetu Care, we bring eye health services directly to communities. Through mobile medical outreaches, we provide eye screenings, free reading and prescription glasses, and referrals for surgical procedures such as cataract removal. By detecting issues early and connecting patients to the right care, we align with the International Agency for the Prevention of Blindness (IAPB) to help prevent avoidable blindness and preserve dignity.

Our mission is to ensure that sight is protected, and no one loses their vision simply because care is too far away.

Aging with Dignity

Aging with Dignity

As younger generations migrate to cities in search of work, many older persons are left living alone, managing chronic illness, limited mobility, and financial strain. Accessing healthcare often requires long travel distances, physical strength, and resources they simply do not have.

At Kwetu Care, we intentionally include elderly men and women in our outreach programs. We provide screening, treatment, medication, and referrals tailored to their needs — particularly for chronic conditions such as hypertension, diabetes, arthritis, and vision problems.

We believe aging should not mean neglect. It should be accompanied by care, respect, and access to the services that allow individuals to live their later years with comfort and dignity.

Chronic Disease Care

Chronic Disease Care and Longterm Support

The Silent Rise of Non-Communicable Diseases

Across rural communities, conditions such as hypertension and diabetes are increasing steadily. Unlike infectious diseases, these illnesses often develop quietly. Many people live for years without knowing they are affected until serious complications occur.

When left unmanaged, high blood pressure can lead to stroke, heart failure, or kidney damage. Diabetes, without consistent care, can result in blindness, nerve damage, and other life-altering complications. These outcomes are largely preventable with early diagnosis, regular monitoring, and consistent medication.

Yet for many families, access to screening and ongoing treatment remains limited because of distance and finances. The World Health Organization warns that noncommunicable diseases (NCDs) disproportionately affect low- and middle-income countries, where interventions are often the hardest to reach.

Through our medical outreaches, we actively screen for diabetes and hypertension, initiate treatment where needed, and provide essential medication. Beyond the outreach, we connect patients to partner clinics and structured support groups to ensure continuity of care.

Chronic illness requires more than a one-time intervention. It requires follow-up, education, and a support system.

Combatting Preventable Mortality in Rural Kenya

"The geography of a patient should not determine their survival."

In many rural communities across Laikipia, simple geography is often a death sentence. Diseases like malaria, pneumonia, and diarrhea remain the leading causes of mortality, despite being entirely preventable and treatable with basic medicine. For a mother in a remote village, the nearest clinic might be a four-hour walk away. Kwetu Care bridges this specific gap through our Mobile Diagnostic Hubs, effectively bringing the hospital to the doorstep of the most vulnerable.

The "Last Mile" Health Challenge

The concept of the "Last Mile" is usually applied to logistics, but in healthcare, it is a matter of life and death. Traditional healthcare models rely on patients coming to facilities. We flip this model. By deploying motorbike nurses and off-road mobile clinics, we reduce the time-to-treatment from days to minutes. Our teams are equipped with rapid diagnostic kits (RDTs), portable ultrasound tech, and essential antibiotics to identify complications before they become fatal.

Furthermore, we are tackling the crisis of maternal mortality. Complications during pregnancy and childbirth are often detectable early, yet without regular prenatal checkups, they go unnoticed until it is too late. Our mobile units conduct on-site antenatal care, screening for pre-eclampsia, anemia, and fetal distress. By catching these issues early, we can arrange safe transport to higher-level facilities long before an emergency occurs.

Global Data Context:

Every day, over 800 women die from preventable causes related to pregnancy and childbirth. 94% of all maternal deaths occur in low and lower-middle-income countries.

View WHO Maternal Health Data →

Finally, sustainability lies in local empowerment. We don't just treat patients; we train Community Health Volunteers (CHVs). These are trusted members of the village who are trained to recognize danger signs, administer first aid, and manage referrals. By embedding knowledge within the community, we ensure that the fight against preventable death continues even when our mobile trucks act elsewhere. This network of surveillance is our strongest weapon against outbreaks and unchecked chronic conditions.

Education: The Ultimate Vaccine

Knowledge isn't just power; in health, it is survival. Our "Education First" initiative focuses on health literacy, ensuring families understand not just the "how" but the "why" behind hygiene, nutrition, and disease management. When a community understands the germ theory of disease, handwashing stops being a chore and becomes a shield. When a mother understands nutritional groups, a simple meal becomes preventative medicine.

Breaking Cultural Taboos

One of the most significant barriers to rural health is misinformation and cultural stigma. Whether it is hesitation regarding vaccines or dangerous misconceptions about reproductive health, silence costs lives. Kwetu Care uses education to gently but firmly dismantle these barriers. We organize "Health Barazas" (community gatherings) where elders and doctors sit together to discuss health topics openly, merging traditional wisdom with modern medical science to create a consensus that favors life.

We also utilize modern technology to overcome literacy barriers. Our teams carry tablets loaded with visual storytelling apps and mobile-optimized content. This allows us to teach complex concepts—like how a virus spreads or how sugar affects the body—to populations that may not read or write. By using diagrams, videos, and local language voiceovers, we ensure that the message is accessible to everyone, from the grandmother raising her grandkids to the young herdsman in the field.

Sanitation Impact

Proper handwashing and hygiene education can reduce diarrheal diseases by over 40%, saving thousands of children annually.

Sustainable Diet

Teaching communities to utilize drought-resistant local crops (like sorghum and millet) for high-nutrient meals.

Ultimately, our goal is generational change. We run "Health Clubs" in local primary schools where children become Health Ambassadors. They learn about first aid, sanitation, and nutrition, taking these lessons home to their parents. When a child reminds their parent to wash their hands or boil drinking water, the dynamic of the household changes. Education is the only health intervention that pays dividends for decades after the initial investment.

Aging with Dignity and Respect

As the "backbone" of our society, the elderly are guardians of our history and culture. Yet, in traditional aid models, they are often the most overlooked demographic. As urbanization pulls younger generations toward cities, many elderly individuals in rural Laikipia are left isolated, facing the dual burden of poverty and failing health. Our mission at Kwetu Care is to ensure that aging does not mean suffering in silence and that dignity is preserved until the very end.

The Silent Epidemic of NCDs

While infectious diseases get the headlines, Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs) like hypertension, diabetes, and arthritis are the silent killers of the elderly poor. Without regular medication, a manageable condition like high blood pressure leads to strokes that leave elders bedridden and helpless. Our Home-Based Geriatric Care program tackles this head-on. Many of our seniors cannot walk to clinics, so we go to them. Our motorbike nurses deliver chronic medication, provide physical therapy, and monitor vitals on a monthly schedule.

"Helping one senior live without pain is honoring the history of our entire village. We don't just treat bodies; we honor souls."

Beyond physical health, we address the crushing weight of social isolation. Depression is rampant among the rural elderly who feel left behind. Our program includes "Social Check-ins," where volunteers spend time simply talking, listening to stories, and helping with small household chores. We are also piloting intergenerational programs, connecting local youth with elderly "grandparents" for mentorship. This restores a sense of purpose to the elderly, reminding them that they still have value to contribute to the community.

Looking ahead, the demographics of Africa are changing. By 2050, the number of people aged 60 and over will double. Kwetu Care is building the infrastructure for this future now. We are advocating for community-based insurance schemes that cover geriatric care and training families on how to care for aging relatives with compassion. We believe that a society is judged by how it treats its most vulnerable, and we are determined to make Laikipia a model of elder compassion.

Reaching the Last Mile in Ol Moran

In the vast, semi-arid landscapes of Laikipia West, the village of Ol Moran stands as a testament to the "tyranny of distance." For many families here, the nearest sub-county hospital is over 40 kilometers away, often across terrain that is impassable during the rainy season. Our mobile medical outreach was specifically designed to bridge this gap, ensuring that basic primary care is a right, not a geographical privilege.

Our mobile units are rugged, 4x4 clinics on wheels, equipped with cold-chain storage for vaccines, point-of-care testing for malaria and HIV, and a well-stocked pharmacy of essential antibiotics. Every two weeks, our team of clinicians and nurses sets up a temporary station under the shade of acacia trees, serving upwards of 150 patients in a single day who would otherwise have no access to medical expertise.

During our recent intervention in Laikipia, we discovered a significant spike in pediatric respiratory infections. Because our team returns to the same sites regularly, we were able to identify that a local drought had led to increased dust exposure in poorly ventilated dwellings. This allowed us to not only treat the infections but provide "Healthy Home" education to the village elders to mitigate future risks.

Trust is our most vital asset. In remote areas, there is often a deep-seated suspicion of outsiders. By hiring and training Community Health Volunteers (CHVs) from within the local population, we ensure our medical advice is delivered in the native dialect and within a cultural context that resonates with the elders and mothers of the community.

The operational cost of these missions is immense, particularly regarding vehicle maintenance and fuel. However, the cost of inaction is far higher. A preventable infection left untreated in a child can lead to permanent disability or death, devastating a family's economic future. Our mobile clinics are the first line of defense against such tragedies in the most remote corners of the county.

We are currently looking to add a third vehicle to our fleet to expand into the neighboring Baringo border regions. To understand the global challenges of rural medical logistics, we encourage you to read the WHO Fact Sheet on Primary Health Care. Your support keeps our engines running and our medicine chests full.

Maternal Survival in Samburu North

In Samburu North, the ritual of birth is often shadowed by the risk of maternal mortality. High rates of home births without skilled attendants have historically led to preventable complications during delivery. Our foundation’s Maternal Health Program works directly with traditional birth attendants (TBAs) to integrate modern safety protocols without disrespecting cultural heritage.

We provide portable ultrasound screenings—a technology that was once unheard of in these nomadic regions. By identifying high-risk pregnancies, such as placenta previa or breech positioning, weeks in advance, we can arrange for safe transport to the Maralal County Referral Hospital long before a crisis occurs, effectively saving two lives at once.

Nutritional intervention is another key pillar. Anemia remains a leading cause of maternal hemorrhage in the environs of Laikipia. We provide iron supplements and fortified flour to expectant mothers, ensuring they have the physiological strength to endure childbirth. Our "Mama Kits"—containing sterile supplies for delivery—have become a highly valued resource among local women.

Post-natal care is often the most critical window. Many newborn deaths occur within the first 48 hours of life. Our community nurses conduct follow-up visits on motorcycles, reaching mothers who have returned to their bomas (homesteads) to ensure that breastfeeding is established and that no signs of neonatal sepsis are present.

The success of this program is measured in the smiles of healthy toddlers. Since our intervention began, we have seen a 35% increase in facility-based deliveries among our partner communities. This shift is not just about medicine; it is about building a system where every mother feels safe, respected, and empowered during the most vulnerable time of her life.

Our goal is to build a dedicated maternal wing in our base clinic by 2027. To learn more about the global fight for maternal survival, explore the UNICEF Maternal Health Report. Your partnership ensures that no mother in Samburu has to choose between her tradition and her life.

NCD Management: A Clinical Strategy for Hypertension

Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs), specifically hypertension and Type 2 diabetes, have emerged as the leading cause of adult morbidity in rural Laikipia. Historically, medical resources were funnelled toward infectious diseases, leaving a clinical vacuum for long-term physiological care. Our foundation’s NCD strategy is built on a "Continuum of Care" model that moves beyond sporadic screenings into a disciplined, evidence-based management protocol.

We have established community-based clinical hubs that utilize standardized WHO-HEARTS protocols for cardiovascular risk management. By decentralizing blood pressure monitoring to trained Community Health Assistants, we have reduced the rate of undiagnosed Stage 2 hypertension by 45% within our target demographics. This proactive identification is vital to preventing the surge of stroke and renal failure cases that overwhelm regional referral hospitals.

Pharmaceutical consistency is the backbone of our wellness program. In many rural dispensaries, stockouts of essential antihypertensive drugs like Amlodipine or Enalapril are common. Our medical foundation maintains an independent supply chain to ensure that once a patient is titrated onto a medication regimen, they never face the physiological danger of a sudden cessation of treatment due to logistics failures.

We integrate laboratory diagnostics directly into our field outreach. By utilizing point-of-care HbA1c testing and lipid profiling, our clinicians can make immediate, data-backed adjustments to treatment plans. This "real-time" clinical intervention is a significant upgrade from traditional models where patients would wait weeks for laboratory results from urban centers, often losing contact with the provider in the interim.

Education is framed as a clinical necessity rather than a suggestion. We conduct intensive patient seminars on dietary sodium reduction and the metabolic benefits of locally available high-fiber grains. By empowering patients with the biological understanding of their condition, we achieve higher rates of treatment adherence and a significant reduction in long-term hospitalization costs for families.

Looking forward, we are digitizing our patient records to allow for longitudinal tracking of health outcomes. This data is shared with the Ministry of Health to advocate for better NCD resource allocation in rural counties. To explore the latest clinical guidelines on hypertension management in low-resource settings, visit the World Health Organization.

Restoring Vision: The Impact of Surgical Outreach

In the arid regions of Isiolo and Samburu, prolonged exposure to UV radiation and airborne dust leads to some of the highest rates of preventable blindness in the country. Cataracts and trachoma are the primary culprits, often turning productive elders into dependents and placing immense strain on family resources. Our surgical outreach program is dedicated to reversing this trend through high-volume, high-quality eye camps.

Each outreach mission involves a specialized team of ophthalmologists and ophthalmic nurses who convert local dispensaries into sterile operating theaters. By utilizing modern Small Incision Cataract Surgery (SICS) techniques, we can perform dozens of life-altering procedures in a single weekend. The results are immediate; a patient who arrived blind often leaves with functional vision within 24 hours.

Screening is conducted weeks in advance by our Community Health Volunteers. They travel to the most remote bomas to identify candidates who have completely lost their sight. By prioritizing those with bilateral blindness, we focus our resources on patients for whom surgery will have the most profound economic and social impact, restoring their ability to care for themselves and their livestock.

Post-operative care is handled by local clinical teams we have trained in basic ophthalmic hygiene. We provide every surgical patient with protective eyewear and antibiotic drops to ensure zero infection rates post-surgery. This follow-up care is essential in rugged environments where dust and smoke from cooking fires pose a constant threat to a healing eye.

Sustainability is achieved through our "Training the Trainers" initiative. We are currently sponsoring two local nurses for specialized ophthalmic training at the Kenya Medical Training College. Our goal is to ensure that the capacity for eye care remains in these counties long after our surgical teams have moved on to the next location, creating a permanent solution to preventable blindness.

The cost of a single cataract surgery is relatively low, but the return on investment for a human life is immeasurable. To see the global data on eye health and blindness prevention, visit the International Agency for the Prevention of Blindness. Your support allows us to purchase the intraocular lenses that restore sight to the forgotten corners of Kenya.
